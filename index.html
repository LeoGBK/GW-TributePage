Major milestones in the life of George Washington

  • 1732 - Borned in Pop's Creek, Virginia
  • 1749 - Being 17, he was commissioned as the first surveyor of the newly created Culpeper County
  • 1752 - He was appointed adjutant general of the Virginia militia, a rank within the British militia during the colony.
  • 1759 - He married Martha Dandridge Custis.
  • 1765 - The Stamp Act becomes the first direct tax on the colonies, something that he opposed, but without maintaining a leading role.
  • 1774 - The First Continental Congress appointed him as its delegate.
  • 1775 - Washington appeared in the Continental Congress in military uniform, signaling that he was prepared for armed struggle.
  • 1776 - One month after the Declaration of Independence, in the Long Island battle, he leads the fight for the first time as the United States Army.
  • 1781 - Final strike of US Army under his command. After a French naval victory, which allowed American and French forces to trap the British Army in Virginia.
  • 1783 - After the war ended and the Kingdom of Great Britain recognized the United States as an independent republic, he dissolved his army and said goodbye to his soldiers. He resigned his command as Commander-in-Chief and retired to civil life. He was an example of the republican ideal of citizen leadership that rejects power. During this period, the office of President of the United States did not exist.
  • 1787 - He was unanimously elected President of the Philadelphia Convention, participating little in the debates. The delegates designed the presidency (of the United States of America) with Washington in mind, and allowed him to define the position once elected.
  • 1789 - The Electoral College elected him unanimously in the 1789 elections as President of the United States of America. He took the oath of office as the first president under the Constitution of the United States of America on April 30, 1789, in Federal Hall in New York, although, at first, he had not wanted the position.
  • 1792 - He was elected again by the Electoral College for a second presidential term. He is, to date, the only president to receive 100% of the electoral votes.

  • 1797 - After leaving office in March, he returned to Mount Vernon with a deep sense of relief. He spent a lot of time in agriculture.
  • 1799 - He died of pneumonia the night of December 14 at his home at the age of 67.

The first United States Congress voted to pay Washington a salary of $ 25,000 a year, a large sum in 1789. Already wealthy Washington cut the salary, as it valued his image as a disinterested public servant. . At the behest of Congress, however, he eventually accepted the pay, to avoid creating a precedent whereby the presidency would be perceived as an independent position limited only to wealthy individuals who could serve without pay. Washington attended carefully to the pomp and ceremony of office, ensuring that Republican titles and symbols never emulated European royal courts. To that end, he preferred the title "Mr. President" to more majestic suggested treatments. Washington proved to be a skilled administrator. An excellent delegator of duties and a judge of talent and character, he held regular meetings to discuss cabinet issues before making a final decision. In handling routine tasks, he was "systematic, orderly, energetic, concerned with the opinion of others, but decisive, determined to the general objectives and with coherence in the particular actions with them". Washington reluctantly served a second term as president. He refused to run for a third party, thus establishing the customary policy of up to two terms for a president, which was later signed into law by the 22nd Amendment to the Constitution.
